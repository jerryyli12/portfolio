Subtheme: Exclusionary Zoning

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. Which regions in Boston have the most exclusionary zoning? This question is motivated by the New York Times visualizations, which show the single-family zoning in other cities across the US, and it's also generally the primary focus of this subtheme.
  2. How do demographics differ in areas zoned for single-family housing vs. not; e.g., who is being "excluded" by exclusionary zoning? The provided reading "Exclusionary by Design" argues that zoning has, throughout history, been used as a tool of race and class exclusion in Boston. With this question, I hope to determine if the data continues to support this argument.
  3. Where might there be the best opportunities for upzoning? This question is motivated by personal interest and past experience. In 11.001, my group visited Alewife - the terminus of the red line - and argued in favor of transit-oriented, affordable development in the area. I'm curious what the data says regarding opportunities for upzoning in this region and in others in Boston.

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

In summary, this exploratory visual analysis serves as a preliminary investigation into the Boston single-family zoning dataset. I first found that - unlike in other cities - Boston's single-family zoned areas are less patterned spatially, and the amount of land zoned for single-family homes differs wildly from municipality to municipality. I then observed that exclusionary zoning in Boston disproportionately affects lower-income, African-American and Latino populations. The trend held overall and across individual municipalities. Furthermore, I established that areas in Boston which allow multifamily development are higher in density, and that higher density correlates with more diversity in income - even when controlling for the zoning type. Finally, by looking at exclusionary-zoned MBTA communities with high income and high density, I took a first step toward understanding where upzoning might help most.
