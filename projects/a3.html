Phase One: Sketching Visualizations

For this phase, I propose a broad motivating question: how has the US population distribution changed from 1900 to 2000, and do the changes differ by gender?

Design Rationale for Sketch 1:

  • This type of visualization was the first to come to mind, as it's a common and effective way to depict a population distribution. The reader can clearly see the distribution over both age and gender.
  • I chose to include 1900 and 2000 on the same plot to depict the change over time, using an absolute x-axis. This allows the reader to see the absolute change in each bin, while still allowing the individual distributions to be seen.
  • Overall, I think this sketch is very effective and helps answer my question: the US population is aging, with much more of the population concentrated in the "middle-age" area. Two areas where this visualization falls short is that the absolute (un-normalized) x-axis makes it difficult to compare percentages, which the reader is probably more interested in instead. The second is that it's hard to see differences in gender here, as the distributions really aren't that different. These issues were the target focus of my second sketch.

Design Rationale for Sketch 2:

  • In this sketch, I hoped to focus exclusively on relative (i.e. percent) changes in the distribution. I also hoped to see clearer differences by gender.
  • This visualization is able to communicate what age groups (and of which genders) increased the most in population relative to each other. And, if the reader is interested in comparing changes by gender, it's easier to do so as they are directly adjacent (rather than having to compare non-adjacent bars in the first sketch).
  • However, this sketch lost a lot of information pertinent to the original question. While we can see the change in the population distribution, we cannot see either of the individual distributions from 1900 or 2000, nor can we see any absolute figures/changes. Moreover, as we discussed in lecture, color saturation is probably not the most effective mark to use here, making this visualization harder to read than the bars of sketch 1. Hence, for the last sketch, I decided to revisit a closer design to sketch 1 while keeping the lessons from sketch 2 in mind.

Design Rationale for Sketch 3:

  • For this sketch, I wanted to try to normalize the distributions so the reader can more easily make relative comparisons between 1900 and 2000. I also decided to omit gender, as I wanted to focus on the age distribution.
  • This visualization does the job, clearly allowing the reader to answer a question such as "how much more of the population is of retirement age in 2000 vs. 1900"?
  • Nonetheless, I still felt flaws with this visualization. The circular shape is a debatable choice, since age is not truly cyclic (though you could argue something about the "circle of life" here). Moreover, the circular marks simply felt much more difficult to read; it's a lot harder to compare between, say, the 0-4 age group and the 45-49 age group compared to sketch 1.

Reflection

In summary, I sketched three different designs, a population pyramid, a heatmap, and a radial pie chart. The population pyramid is generally easy to read and to make comparisons (especially between age groups), and it also shows the population distributions for both 1900 and 2000, which can help the reader draw additional observations. However, it lacks in showing relative differences over time due to the absolute x-axis. The heatmap was the solution to clearly show relative differences, so the reader could easily answer something like "is more of the population today composed of adults than in 1900?" However, the heatmap lost much of the important information, such as the individual population distributions themselves, and the choice of mark made it difficult to read. Finally, the radial pie chart was a move back toward sketch 1, but now still allowing the reader to easily make normalized, relative comparisons between 1900 and 2000. However, like sketch 2, I felt it was more difficult to read and made some comparisons between age groups more difficult. Hence, my general thought going into the next phase was to follow the general design of sketch 1, while potentially synthesizing some aspects of the latter two sketches to improve upon it.

Phase Two: Final Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

For the final visualization, I decided to focus in on a more specific question than before: how much older is the US population growing? Essentially, I wanted to make a visualization which best showed how the population age distribution has changed, with much more of the population being middle-age and older adults, and much less of the population being children. I chose to use a type of visualization commonly known as a population pyramid, which is essentially two bar charts facing opposite directions. This is the best choice for the data because of the binned age variable and the fact that bars are the most effective mark for comparison across age bins. The reader can clearly see which age groups are more or less represented in the population, enabling the identification of historical baby booms. Furthermore, I chose to use two separate facets for the temporal variable, 1900 vs. 2000. This is because, in my opinion, it is the best way to allow for both relative and absolute comparison across time (the x-axis displays absolute values, and the reader can see the relative shapes of the distributions clearly). Even at a brief glance, the reader can immediately see that much more of the population in 2000 is comprised of middle-age adults, rather than being predominantly children as in 1900. In terms of more minor design choices, I opted to use a common y-axis to make reading the visualization seamless. Keeping a linear scale for the x-axis and sorting the age bins are additional common-sense choices for the data.

Despite these design efforts, my visualization does obscure and/or ineffectively represent some aspects of the data. Firstly, comparison between gender is difficult. As we discussed in lecture, humans are not very good at comparing bar lengths with separate x-axes, which the reader is forced to do for comparing male and female bars. I accepted this as a "flaw" because I decided to focus on age, rather than gender, for my visualization. The second issue is that the x-axis scale between the facets are different. This means that, if the reader is not careful, they may not realize that the bars on the right actually represent approximately 2.5x more people than the bars on the left, reflecting the large overall increase in the US population. I also accepted this as a "flaw" because I again wanted to focus on the change in the population distribution, not the absolute population numbers. Using a different scale for the x-axis enables the quick relative observations aforementioned.

Reflection

Sketching definitely helped me brainstorm for the final design. Coming in, I knew that a population pyramid like sketch 1 would probably be the most effective, and sketch 1 definitely reinforced to me its effectiveness. However, my initial population pyramid sketch also helped me notice some flaws in the design, such as the difficulty in making relative comparisons between 1900 and 2000, as well as the ineffective representation of differences between gender. The heatmap was an attempt to address both of these issues, but ultimately lost too much of the original information and readability. It helped me realize that I ought to redirect my motivating question on age rather than trying to tackle both on age and gender together. Finally, the radial pie chart gave me insights into how I should make relative comparisons in the population distribution from 1900 to 2000 easy. It led me to realize that, while I wanted to use a normalized, shared scale for both the 1900 and 2000 distributions, plotting them on top of each other was not the most effective choice. Ultimately, these observations led me to my final visualization, where I kept most of the aspects from sketch 1, but faceted the pyramid temporally due to my observations from my additional sketches.
